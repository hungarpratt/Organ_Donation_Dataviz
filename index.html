Organ Donation in America

Who Donates — or Doesn't — and Why?

The Organ Shortage

In the United States today, roughly 100,000 people are waiting for a donated organ, and thousands die each year before a matching organ becomes available. Although animal-to-human transplants and living kidney donation are expanding, the vast majority of organs still come from deceased individuals who consented to donate at the time of their death.

Today, all fifty states offer drivers license applicant the option to register as organ donors. However, only half of Americans have chosen to do so, leaving the organ donation system under-supplied.

Media Misinformation

A spate of recent articles in highly respected publications have further exacerbated the shortfall. In Feb 2025, The New York Times published an article questioning the integrity and equity of the U.S. donation system. Several months later, the same paper published “Doctors Were Preparing To Remove Their Organs. Then They Woke Up.” (Article later emoved from website.)

Impact on Donor Rates

These articles prompted thousands of registered donors to remove themselves from state registries. According to a letter issued by the Association of Organ Procurement Organizations, “These stories have directly led to the biggest increase in people removing themselves from donor registries ever recorded, putting patients waiting for transplants at greater risk.”

Leading with Insights

To counter the misinformation fueled by these stories, the donor-advocacy community needs clear, evidence-based messaging. That requires understanding why some people register as donors while others hesitate, and how their beliefs shape those decisions. Addressing these factors directly can help restore trust and support the lifesaving goals of the donation system.

National Survey
