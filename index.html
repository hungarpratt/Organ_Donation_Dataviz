Organ Donation in the United States

Who Donates — or Doesn't — And Why?

The Organ Shortage

In the United States today, roughly 100,000 people are waiting for a donated organ, and thousands die each year before a matching organ becomes available. Today, all fifty states offer applicants for drivers licenses the option to register as an organ donor. However, only half of Americans have chosen to do so, leaving the organ donation system woefully under-supplied.

Media Misinformation

A spate of recent articles in highly respected publications have further exacerbated the shortfall. In Feb 2025, The New York Times published an article questioning the integrity and equity of the U.S. donation system. Several months later, the same paper published “Doctors Were Preparing To Remove Their Organs. Then They Woke Up.” (Article removed from website.)

Impact on Donor Rates

These articles prompted thousands of registered donors to remove themselves from state registries. According to a letter issued by the Association of Organ Procurement Organizations, “These stories have directly led to the biggest increase in people removing themselves from donor registries ever recorded, putting patients waiting for transplants at greater risk.”

Insights That Elevate Public Messaging

To counter misinformation and sensationalized reporting, the donor-advocacy community needs public messaging that communicates the truth with clarity and care. Achieving this begins with a deeper understanding of why some people choose to register as donors in the event of death while others do not.

By examining the differences between these groups—and the beliefs that shape their decisions—we can rebuild trust, strengthen donor-registration rates, and better meet the growing demand for lifesaving transplants.
